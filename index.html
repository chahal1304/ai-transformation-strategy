Step 2 — Opportunity Mapping

From the two audits, we build a prioritised list of use cases. Each one is scored on business impact (revenue gained, cost saved, time recovered, risk reduced) × feasibility (data readiness, integration complexity, time to deploy).

This is not a list of "AI things we could build." It's a list of specific business problems, ranked by what they're worth to solve and how quickly we can solve them. A typical diagnosis produces 12–18 use cases. We pick the top 2–3 for the pilot — the ones where impact is high, data is ready, and the system can be live in 8–12 weeks.

Pillar 1 — Product Scalability: Input Front
Deeper knowledge layer for Building Company Intelligence

The pilot connected structured data. Scale adds the rest — unstructured data from past launches, experiments, failures, and bad calls; and decisions, meaning why the company chose a path and the context and trade-offs behind major strategic calls.

We set SOPs, assign owners, run monthly audits, and build systems to extract knowledge from existing documents — so what the company already knows becomes something the intelligence can actually use.

Example — Decision log after 6 months

"You're evaluating a Diwali bundle. Last year's Diwali bundle: CAC 38% above monthly average, LTV of acquired customers 26% below annual average, margin compressed 9 points because the 25% discount didn't account for seasonal CAC inflation. Net: revenue-positive, contribution-margin-negative. Two years ago, a 15% discount with gift-with-purchase: 40% lower volume but contribution-positive."

That context exists in your company right now. It's in someone's head or a slide deck nobody's opened since January.

Example: Q3 2023 — Scaled Meta on Coffee Protein Bar without refreshing creatives. CAC rose from ₹390 to ₹580 over 14 weeks. ₹35L in excess spend before it was reversed. Lesson: creative fatigue shows in CTR weeks before ROAS moves. Watch CTR weekly, not monthly.
Decision Rationale
Why the company chose a path — the context and trade-offs behind a major strategic call.
Example: Removed free shipping on all orders in Q1 2024. Contribution margin on sub-₹800 orders was negative after courier costs. Free shipping now only above ₹999. Reduced order volume 8% but improved blended CM by 4pp within 6 weeks.

Pillar 3 — How the Team Changes

The system changes what people do all day. That needs to be designed, not assumed. The usual outcome when it isn't: the team uses it occasionally, defaults to old habits within a month, and the founder ends up being the only one actually working with the outputs.

Three things need to happen.

We train people

To drive adoption, people have to find it useful first. That means training isn't about the software — it's about learning how to engage with what the intelligence surfaces. Most teams default to pulling numbers. The shift is from "where's the number" to "what does this mean and what do we do."

Data question
"What was CAC last week?"
Reasoning question
"CAC increased 18% this week. What's driving it and is growth still contribution-margin positive?"
Data question
"What's our inventory status?"
Reasoning question
"Which SKUs run out before next inbound, what's the revenue at risk, and are any being pushed by active ad spend?"
Data question
"Show me reviews for the new serum."
Reasoning question
"What are the top 3 complaints in reviews and tickets, do they correlate with a specific batch, and how does this compare to our last 3 launches?"
Redefine what each role does

We sit with each functional lead and map what the intelligence now covers versus what they still own. One page per role, half a day per function. Without this, people either duplicate what the system is already doing or hand off decisions they shouldn't.

Can't we just build this internally?

The interface is easy. The hard part is what goes behind it — cleaning messy data, capturing what people know but haven't written down, mapping how decisions actually get made. Most internal AI projects stall here: they connect a model to some documents and call it done. Without structured company context, the answers could apply to any business. Your team can build a chatbot. The question is whether they should spend months figuring out what we've already solved across multiple D2C brands.
