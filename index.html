Section 01 · Thesis

Most D2C brands don't have a data problem.
They have a decision problem.

Information is scattered across Shopify, Meta, your 3PL, your finance sheet, and a dozen WhatsApp threads. No one has the full picture at the moment a decision needs to be made.

The result: decisions based on incomplete information, followed by slow correction when something goes wrong.

How most teams work today

Manual and reactive
  • –teams gather data
  • –discuss in meetings
  • –make decisions
  • –hope they were right

What most "AI tools" deliver

A better dashboard
  • –combine data
  • –generate dashboards
  • –make suggestions

What we build

A decision layer on top of your business
  • –understands context
  • –pulls every relevant signal
  • –recommends actions
  • –explains why
  • –tracks outcomes
  • –learns over time

Why most AI projects stall before they scale

  • 01

    The work doesn't change. AI gets added on top of old processes. The bottleneck was never the data — it was how decisions got made. That part stays broken.

  • 02

    It gives outputs, not decisions. A recommendation that says "ROAS is strong, consider scaling" doesn't tell you whether scaling will cause a stockout in 9 days. Outputs without business context aren't useful.

  • 03

    The people who need to act never get involved. Insights are generated, reviewed by analysts, and summarized in slides that arrive too late. By then, the moment to act has passed.

  • 04

    There's no feedback loop. The system doesn't know whether its suggestions worked. So it keeps making the same kind of recommendation, whether or not it's right.

  • 05

    It never leaves the pilot. One use case works, everyone calls it a success, and nothing else changes. The system stays a tool one person checks occasionally instead of something the company runs on.

Phase 01 Weeks 1–3

Diagnosis

Before we build anything, we need to understand how decisions are actually made — not how they're supposed to be made. This phase maps your decision patterns, audits your data, and identifies where the highest-value use cases are.

Audit

Audit 01 · Decision Audit

We sit with you and document the 15–25 recurring decisions you make as a founder.

We document

1. Decision Making

  • Every recurring decision the founder makes
  • What information the founder uses
  • Where that info comes from
  • How long it takes to gather
  • How confident they are in the outcome

2. Decision Execution

  • Who is responsible for carrying it out
  • How it gets communicated to the relevant team
  • How long execution takes after the decision is made
  • Whether there is a tracking mechanism or it is assumed to happen
  • Where execution typically breaks down or slows
  • Whether the outcome is reviewed after the fact and by whom

Why it matters: if we don't understand how a decision is made today, we can't build something that meaningfully improves it.

Inventory Risk

At current sell-through, stock lasts 8.8 days. Inbound PO arrives in 22. A 20% spend increase exhausts stock in 6 days — leaving a 16-day gap with a live campaign and no product. Last stockout caused CAC to rise 34% and it didn't recover until stock returned.

Margin Risk

The active bundle is discounted 40%, bringing contribution margin to 11% vs 28% on non-promoted SKUs. Scaling buys revenue the CAC cannot recover at current economics.

Cohort Quality Risk

60-day repeat rate on Meta-acquired customers: 18%, vs 26% blended. CAC payback: 9+ months. More spend acquires more of the same low-quality cohort.

Procurement Risk

Supplier reprices +16% on June 28. Today's margin economics are not next month's. Either bundle price rises or margin falls further — there's no clean exit once scaling begins.

Governance

Autonomy expands one use case at a time. The controls have to keep pace. The gap between what the system is doing and what governance covers is where risk accumulates — invisibly, until a bad outcome makes it visible. We treat governance as a first-class deliverable, not an afterthought.

Data Governance

Who can access what data, and what quality standards it must meet.

  • Finance sees financial data; Marketing sees campaign data; Ops sees inventory data
  • Every data source has a named owner responsible for its accuracy
  • Vendor pricing and supplier contracts are classified as confidential
  • Outdated or unapproved data cannot be used for decision-making
AI Governance

What the system can recommend, what requires approval, and what it cannot do.

  • Large budget changes require human approval
  • High-impact inventory decisions require Ops sign-off
  • Legal, HR, and other out-of-scope topics are blocked
  • Low-confidence recommendations go to review, not to action
Operational Governance

Clear rules for approvals, escalations, and exceptions — so accountability is never ambiguous.

  • Inventory decisions above threshold escalate to Ops Lead
  • Campaign pauses above ₹2L require founder sign-off
  • When supplier data is missing, procurement is escalated — the system does not assume
  • Every approved recommendation is logged with decision-maker, timestamp, and outcome

Section 03 · Conclusion

Our goal isn't to help you use AI.

Our goal is to help you build a company that makes better decisions, runs better processes, and continuously improves over time.

Every important decision should:

  • *use the right data and context,
  • *explain its reasoning,
  • *have a clear owner,
  • *be part of a well-designed process,
  • *be tracked and reviewed,
  • *and improve from past outcomes.

That's what we mean by AI transformation — not just adding AI tools, but redesigning decisions, processes, and ways of working so the business gets better over time.

FAQ
Can't we just build this internally?

The interface is the easy part. What takes 6–9 months to figure out is the layer underneath it: cleaning messy data across five tools that all tell different stories, mapping how decisions are actually made (not how they're supposed to be made), building the feedback loop that makes the system smarter over time, and capturing the institutional knowledge your team carries in their heads.

Most internal AI projects stall at the second step. They connect a model to some documents and stop there. Without structured company context, the output is generic enough to apply to any D2C brand — which means it's not actually useful for yours.

Your team can build a chatbot. The question is whether they should spend months solving problems we've already worked through across multiple D2C businesses.

Why not use another AI vendor?

Most AI vendors help you deploy tools. We build the decision layer those tools need to be useful.

  • Chatbots and workflow automation

  • Generic outputs for any business
What we build
  • Decision intelligence for D2C founders

  • Connected company context — SKUs, suppliers, customers, channels, memory
  • Source-backed recommendations grounded in your data
We already have dashboards. Why do we need this?

Dashboards tell you what happened.

They show charts, metrics, and trends — but they still expect someone to:

  • –interpret the data,
  • –gather missing context,
  • –discuss options with the team,
  • –make a decision, and
  • –follow through on execution.

The hard part isn't seeing the numbers. It's deciding what to do next.

Our system is built around decisions, not dashboards. It doesn't just combine data from different systems. It combines data with business context, company knowledge, and past decisions to answer questions.

Over time, the system also learns from outcomes. It knows which recommendations worked, which didn't, and improves future decisions accordingly.

Instead of showing It tells you
Revenue is down 12%. Revenue is down because repeat purchases have dropped in the North region after a stockout. Based on current inventory and demand forecasts, increasing ad spend is likely to worsen the problem. Prioritise replenishment first and delay campaign expansion by five days.

A dashboard is a reporting tool.
This is a decision system.

How is this different from ChatGPT connected to our documents?

Connecting a model to your documents gives you document search. That's useful, but it's not decision intelligence.

ChatGPT on your docs
  • Finds what a document says
  • No memory of past decisions or their outcomes
  • No awareness of live business constraints
  • Generic answers that could apply anywhere
This system
  • Connects information to a specific decision
  • Surfaces relevant past decisions and what happened
  • Knows your inventory, margins, supplier risk, and channel performance
  • Answers are source-backed and specific to your business
Our data is too messy to start.

That's exactly why this needs to happen. Most D2C brands aren't AI-ready on day one — Shopify, Meta, GA4, HubSpot, and your finance sheet all tell slightly different stories. That's normal.

The first step isn't to build a decision system. It's to make your data reliable enough to trust. You don't need perfect data to start — you need a clear path to get there. That's where the work begins.
